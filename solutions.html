Problem 1

Express \(\begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix}\) as a linear combination of \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\).

Solution:

We need to find scalars \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) such that:

\(c_1\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix} + c_2\begin{bmatrix}2\\0\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}7\\5\end{bmatrix}\)

This gives us the system:

\(1c_1 + 2c_2 = 7\)

\(1c_1 + 0c_2 = 5\)

From the second equation: \(c_1 = 5\)

Substitute into first equation: \(5 + 2c_2 = 7\) \(\Rightarrow 2c_2 = 2\) \(\Rightarrow c_2 = 1\)

Answer: \(5\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix} + 1\begin{bmatrix}2\\0\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}7\\5\end{bmatrix}\)

Problem 2

Solve using linear combination:

\(\begin{cases} 3x + 2y = 7 \\ x - y = 1 \end{cases}\)

Solution:

Multiply the second equation by 2:

\(2x - 2y = 2\)

Now add to the first equation:

\(3x + 2y = 7\)

\(2x - 2y = 2\)

_________

\(5x = 9\) \(\Rightarrow x = \frac{9}{5}\)

Substitute back into second equation:

\(\frac{9}{5} - y = 1\) \(\Rightarrow y = \frac{4}{5}\)

Answer: \(x = \frac{9}{5}, y = \frac{4}{5}\)

Problem 3

Can \(\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}\) be written as a linear combination of \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}\)?

Solution:

We need to check if there exist \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) such that:

\(c_1\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix} + c_2\begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}2\\3\end{bmatrix}\)

This gives the system:

\(c_1 + c_2 = 2\)

\(c_1 + 2c_2 = 3\)

Subtract first equation from second:

\(c_2 = 1\)

Then \(c_1 = 1\)

Check: \(1\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix} + 1\begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}2\\3\end{bmatrix}\) âœ“

Answer: Yes, with coefficients \(c_1 = 1\) and \(c_2 = 1\)

Problem 4

Can \(\begin{bmatrix} 5 \\ 3 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}\) be written as a linear combination of \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\), \(\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}\), and \(\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 2 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\)?

Solution:

Let \(c_1\), \(c_2\), and \(c_3\) be scalars such that:

\[ c_1\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\\1\end{bmatrix} + c_2\begin{bmatrix}0\\1\\2\end{bmatrix} + c_3\begin{bmatrix}2\\2\\0\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}5\\3\\4\end{bmatrix} \]

This leads to the system of equations:

\[ \begin{aligned} c_1 + 2c_3 &= 5 \quad \text{(1)} \\ c_2 + 2c_3 &= 3 \quad \text{(2)} \\ c_1 + 2c_2 &= 4 \quad \text{(3)} \end{aligned} \]

From equation (1):

\[ c_1 = 5 - 2c_3 \tag{4} \]

From equation (2):

\[ c_2 = 3 - 2c_3 \tag{5} \]

Substitute equations (4) and (5) into equation (3):

\[ (5 - 2c_3) + 2(3 - 2c_3) = 4 \] \[ 5 - 2c_3 + 6 - 4c_3 = 4 \] \[ 11 - 6c_3 = 4 \] \[ -6c_3 = -7 \Rightarrow c_3 = \frac{7}{6} \]

Now substitute back to find \(c_1\) and \(c_2\):

\[ c_1 = 5 - 2c_3 = 5 - \frac{14}{6} = \frac{30}{6} - \frac{14}{6} = \frac{16}{6} = \frac{8}{3} \] \[ c_2 = 3 - 2c_3 = 3 - \frac{14}{6} = \frac{18}{6} - \frac{14}{6} = \frac{4}{6} = \frac{2}{3} \]

Final answer:

\[ \boxed{ c_1 = \frac{8}{3}, \quad c_2 = \frac{2}{3}, \quad c_3 = \frac{7}{6} } \]

Yes, the vector \(\begin{bmatrix}5\\3\\4\end{bmatrix}\) can be written as a linear combination of the given vectors.

Problem 5

Find \(3\begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix} + 2\begin{bmatrix}3\\1\end{bmatrix}\).

Solution:

\(3\begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}3\\6\end{bmatrix}\)

\(2\begin{bmatrix}3\\1\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}6\\2\end{bmatrix}\)

Sum: \(\begin{bmatrix}3\\6\end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix}6\\2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}9\\8\end{bmatrix}\)

Answer: \(\begin{bmatrix}9\\8\end{bmatrix}\)

Problem 6

Given that \(\vec{a} = \begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix}\) and \(\vec{b} = \begin{bmatrix}3\\4\end{bmatrix}\). Plot \(2\vec{a} + \vec{b}\) on a graph.

Solution:

First calculate \(2\vec{a} + \vec{b}\):

\(2\begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}2\\4\end{bmatrix}\)

\(\begin{bmatrix}2\\4\end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix}3\\4\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}5\\8\end{bmatrix}\)

The resulting vector is \(\begin{bmatrix}5\\8\end{bmatrix}\).

On a graph, this would be an arrow from (0,0) to (5,8).
